# V.CONCLUSION

PMFCs are apromising technology in the field of energy harvesting,and EIS measurements have largely been used to characterize and improve MFCs.Unfortunately,costly EIS workstations limit impedance spectra monitoring upon deployment.Thiswork has pioneered an innovative ultralow power EIS-monitoring technology powered by a PMFC. This breakthrough opens up exciting new possibilities for the use of PMFCs as biosensors.In a practical and novel self-powered sensing approach,the same reactor serves as both an energy source and an EIS biosensor.EIS measurements are triggered when energy levels are sufficient and then communicated to a base station throughLoRa,demonstrating the potential forreal-world application.We also propose to periodically detach the EHS from thecell to let it recover, in an intermittent harvesting approach apt to maximize PMFCs’ power production window. We then assessed the ultralow power capabilities of the MAX30134, which successfully matched reference potentiostat results,and evaluated the consumption of a full-frequency range EIS sweep at $E _ { \mathrm { E I S } } = 3 . 6 4 \mathrm { J }$ ,proving that ultralow-power EIS is compatible with PMFC power production.Our research has demonstrated the significant variations of the impedance spectra across time (three days) and different activity levels (active and inactive) of PMFCs.This finding underscores the potential relevance of EIS inassessingplant health.

Future work will explore this cost-effective infrastructure for collecting dense crop data upon deployment and developing acomprehensive link between plant health and EIS measurements.
